Frame 01 / 04
⚙️
Structural
The organisation as a machine. Every part has a purpose. When the machine breaks down, look at the design first.
Lens: Engineer / Architect
The core question
"Is the problem caused by unclear roles, broken processes, or misaligned goals?"
The structural frame looks at how work is organised and coordinated. It focuses on goals, roles, rules, policies, technology and environment. A structural problem is not about people being difficult — it's about the design being unclear or misaligned.
When in doubt: draw the org chart and map the process
Signals this frame applies
What does a structural problem look like?
🔁
The same mistakes keep happeningNobody writes them down. No process catches them. It's not stupidity — it's missing structure.
🤷
Two people own the same thing — or nobody doesOverlap and gaps in responsibility. Salesforce queue not distributed. Tasks assigned to "the team."
📋
Goals exist but nobody agrees on prioritiesEveryone is busy. Nothing strategic gets done. Individual effort is high; collective output is low.
KILROY Scenarios
Recognise any of these?
A TA is consistently sending offers late — even after you've raised it twice.
Structural lens asks: Is the response-time expectation documented? Is it in their onboarding? Is there a Salesforce view that tracks response time daily?
Your pipeline in Salesforce is always messy. Requests sit in the wrong stage for weeks.
Structural lens asks: Is there a documented pipeline hygiene protocol? Who is responsible for reviewing it — and how often?
Marketing runs a campaign. Sales gets flooded and the lead quality is terrible.
Structural lens asks: Is there a brief/handoff process between marketing and sales before a campaign launches?
If this frame applies — act here
Structural interventions
01
Clarify the role, not the person. Write down what "done well" looks like. Make expectations explicit — not assumed.
02
Build the process before you coach the behaviour. If you keep correcting the same thing, it's a design problem.
03
Use your data. The Salesforce operations dashboard is a structural X-ray. Overdue tasks, unassigned cases — these are structural signals.
The trap
When structural thinking goes wrong
Adding more process to solve a motivation problem. Creating a rule when what's needed is a conversation. Over-structuring a small team that works better on trust and autonomy.

The structural frame is necessary but not sufficient. A perfectly designed org with people who don't believe in what they're doing is a beautiful machine nobody wants to run.
Frame 02 / 04
🤝
Human Resource
The organisation as a family. People bring needs, feelings and ambitions to work. Ignore this and the talent walks — or stays, disengaged.
Lens: Coach / Servant Leader
The core question
"Are people's skills, needs and motivations matched to what the organisation is asking of them?"
The HR frame focuses on the fit between people and the organisation. When people are energised, trusted, developed and well-matched to their roles, performance follows. When they feel unseen or misused, performance suffers — no matter how good the process is.
When in doubt: have the real conversation you've been putting off
Signals this frame applies
What does an HR problem look like?
📉
A previously strong performer drops offWin rate down. Offers slow. No obvious process change. Something shifted personally or relationally.
🎭
Technically compliant, but no energyThey do what's asked. Minimum effort. Never goes beyond. The data looks okay; the reality doesn't.
🔄
High turnover despite decent payPeople leave managers, not companies. If someone good is leaving, which unmet need drove them out?
KILROY Scenarios
Recognise any of these?
A TA's Win Rate has dropped from 38% to 22% over three months. Pipeline looks normal. No customer complaints.
HR lens asks: When did you last have a genuine check-in — not about pipeline, but about how they're doing? What's changed in their life or role recently?
You gave clear feedback in a 1-1. Nothing changed. You gave it again. Still nothing.
HR lens asks: Is the feedback landing the way you intend it? Do they feel safe enough to admit they don't know how to fix it? Is fear blocking action?
Your best TA has just started looking at other opportunities. You only found out through someone else.
HR lens asks: What need — growth, recognition, autonomy, challenge — is not being met? And why didn't they feel safe telling you?
If this frame applies — act here
Human resource interventions
01
Ask before you tell. "What's making this difficult?" before "Here's what you need to do." Most people know what they should do. Motivation is the actual problem.
02
Develop, don't just manage. A TA who feels like they're growing will outperform a TA who feels they're being monitored.
03
Match the person to the role, not vice versa. Not everyone should be doing the same things. Play to strengths deliberately.
The trap
When HR thinking goes wrong
Prioritising harmony over accountability. Avoiding hard conversations because they feel "unkind." Treating every performance problem as a motivation issue when sometimes the person is simply in the wrong role.

Empathy without accountability is just tolerance of underperformance. The HR frame works best when paired with the structural frame — care AND clarity.
Frame 03 / 04
⚡
Political
The organisation as an arena. Resources are scarce. People have competing interests. Power is real — and pretending it isn't doesn't make it go away.
Lens: Negotiator / Strategist
The core question
"Whose interests are at stake? Who has power — and are they aligned or in conflict?"
The political frame views organisations as coalitions of individuals with different interests and unequal power. Conflict is normal — not a sign of dysfunction. The skill is learning to navigate it: build alliances, manage competing agendas, and find solutions that work across interests.
When in doubt: ask who benefits, who loses, and who has influence
Signals this frame applies
What does a political problem look like?
🧊
Decisions get made but never implementedEveryone nodded in the meeting. Nothing changed. Someone with influence is quietly blocking it.
💥
Two strong performers keep clashingBoth are talented. Together they create friction. Their interests or status are in competition.
🔕
Your initiative keeps dying quietlyNo one openly opposes it. But it never gets resources, airtime, or support. Politics is running on a different channel.
KILROY Scenarios
Recognise any of these?
You propose a new way of distributing inbound leads. Two TAs support it. One senior TA goes quiet. It never gets implemented.
Political lens asks: What does the senior TA stand to lose from this change? Have you had a direct conversation about their concern — or assumed they'd come around?
Marketing runs campaigns that Sales doesn't know about in advance. Tension between the two functions is constant.
Political lens asks: Whose KPIs are optimised by the current arrangement? Neither team is "wrong" — they're optimising for different goals. Who has the authority to align them?
A new team member with strong opinions is creating subtle tension with someone who's been there five years.
Political lens asks: The long-timer's informal authority is being challenged. What informal power do they hold? How can you acknowledge their status while making space for fresh energy?
If this frame applies — act here
Political interventions
01
Map the coalition before you launch the initiative. Who needs to be on board? Who can block? Bring them in early — not after the fact.
02
Name the conflict directly. "I think we have a tension here between X and Y — can we talk about it?" is more powerful than pretending it doesn't exist.
03
Find the shared interest. Even opposing parties usually share something. Find the common ground and build from there.
The trap
When political thinking goes wrong
Seeing everything as political manipulation. Becoming cynical about motives. Playing the game so well you lose your own credibility and trust.

The political frame is descriptive, not prescriptive. Acknowledging that power dynamics exist is not the same as endorsing them. The goal is transparency and alignment — not winning at all costs.
Frame 04 / 04
🔥
Symbolic
The organisation as a tribe. Culture is the invisible operating system. Stories, rituals, and symbols shape behaviour more than any policy document ever will.
Lens: Storyteller / Culture Keeper
The core question
"What does it mean to work here? What stories do people tell? What do we celebrate and what do we ignore?"
The symbolic frame focuses on culture, meaning, ritual and identity. It recognises that humans are meaning-making creatures. People don't just work for money — they work for belonging, pride, purpose and story. When culture is broken, no structural fix will solve it.
When in doubt: listen to the stories people tell each other in the corridor
Signals this frame applies
What does a symbolic problem look like?
🌫️
The team hits targets but nobody caresNumbers are fine. Nobody's proud of anything. Work feels transactional. Nobody tells stories about great bookings.
🚪
Strong hires don't lastGood on paper, wrong in the room. Cultural misfit. What is said in interviews vs. what is actually rewarded diverges.
🎭
Values on the wall, not in the room"We care about the customer" — but the team rushes offers, ignores follow-ups. There's a gap between stated and lived values.
KILROY Scenarios
Recognise any of these?
KILROY talks about being travel experts and caring deeply about customers. But your team celebrates the number of bookings — not the quality of the journey they designed.
Symbolic lens asks: What does the team actually celebrate? The metric you reward is the story you're telling about what matters here. Does it match KILROY's identity?
A new TA with great credentials is technically fine but the rest of the team keeps them at arm's length after 4 months.
Symbolic lens asks: Are there unspoken rituals, norms or "how we do things here" that the new person doesn't know? Culture is often invisible to those inside it.
The team went through a difficult period last year. Performance recovered, but the energy never quite came back.
Symbolic lens asks: Has the team processed what happened? Sometimes what's needed is a shared story about "we went through that, and we came back." Culture needs narrative to heal.
If this frame applies — act here
Symbolic interventions
01
Change what you celebrate. Whatever gets recognised gets repeated. Start celebrating the quality of customer interactions — not just the conversion.
02
Tell the story intentionally. "Here's where we were, here's where we are, here's where we're going." Teams that have a shared narrative are more resilient.
03
Make the culture explicit. Your Team Charter from today is a symbolic act — committing together to who you want to be. Use it that way.
The trap
When symbolic thinking goes wrong
Using culture as an excuse to avoid structural problems. "We just need better vibes" when what's needed is clearer roles. Ritual without substance — team lunches and away-days that don't address the underlying issues.

Culture is shaped by every decision you make — who you promote, what you tolerate, what you reward. You're always communicating something symbolically, whether you intend to or not.

Quick Diagnostic — Which Frame?

Read each situation. Pick the primary frame. Then reveal the answer.

Exercise

Same team. Same day. Four problems. Four different root causes.

A
"Our best TA handed in their notice. We had no idea they were unhappy. Their numbers were fine right up until the end."
Which frame explains this — and what should you have done differently?
🤝 Human Resource Frame
Strong performance metrics can mask disengagement. The HR frame would have prompted regular genuine check-ins beyond pipeline reviews — asking about growth, fulfilment, and what's making work hard. Numbers are a lagging indicator of motivation.
B
"We agreed on a new offer template in January. It's now June. Half the team uses it, half doesn't. Nobody knows who's responsible for enforcing it."
Which frame explains this — and what should change?
⚙️ Structural Frame
When a change is agreed but not adopted, the first question is structural: Was it documented and trained? Is there a clear owner? Is compliance visible in Salesforce? Good intentions don't create structural accountability — clear ownership and a review mechanism does.
C
"The team closes every Friday with a quick debrief. When it was cancelled two weeks in a row, the atmosphere shifted noticeably. Nobody said anything, but something felt different."
Which frame explains why this ritual matters?
🔥 Symbolic Frame
The Friday debrief is not just a meeting — it's a ritual that signals "we are a team, we close the week together." Its absence sent a symbolic message, even unintentionally. Small rituals carry significant cultural weight.
D
"A new incentive structure was announced from HQ. It makes sense on paper. Two senior TAs are visibly unenthusiastic. Within a month, newer TAs have followed their lead."
Which frame explains why the rollout is failing?
⚡ Political Frame
The senior TAs hold informal authority. They didn't buy in — possibly because their interests weren't addressed in the design. Newer team members take social cues from high-status peers. The political frame asks: who should have been consulted before the rollout?
E
"Two TAs in the team produce almost identical numbers. One is constantly praised in team meetings. The other never gets mentioned. The unrecognised one starts cutting corners."
Which frame — and is there more than one at play?
🤝 HR + 🔥 Symbolic (both)
HR frame: An unmet need for recognition is draining motivation. Symbolic frame: What you praise in public is a signal to the whole team about what is valued. Inconsistency in recognition communicates a hierarchy of worth that corrodes culture.
F
"Your market's Win Rate has been flat for 8 months despite coaching, training and multiple process changes. Everything you try makes a small dent then fades."
Which frame haven't you tried yet?
🔥 Symbolic Frame
If structural and HR interventions haven't moved the needle, ask what story the team is telling itself. "We're just not a high-conversion market." "It's always been like this." Culture can absorb change initiatives without transforming. The story needs to change before the numbers do.

Your diagnosis
Which frame has the most boxes checked?
That's your starting point. Not the only thing — but the most urgent lens to apply when you get home.
The key principle
Great leaders use all four frames.
The goal is not to pick your favourite frame and ignore the others. It's to ask: which frame am I underusing? That's usually where the blind spot lives.
One action
This week, look at your team through the frame you use least.
Structural → Map a broken process.
HR → Have the check-in you've been avoiding.
Political → Name the conflict you've been dancing around.
Symbolic → Tell a story about what this team is capable of.
